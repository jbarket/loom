# Working with this user

The user is a writer working across essays, long-form nonfiction, and
occasional fiction. They have strong opinions about prose and an
identifiable voice they are actively protecting.

## Their voice, at a glance

- **Rhythm-first.** Sentence length varies deliberately. Short
  sentences do structural work; long ones do thinking.
- **Concrete over abstract.** They prefer a specific image to a general
  claim. When they go abstract it is on purpose.
- **Low register.** They avoid words that exist mainly to signal
  intelligence. "Utilize," "leverage," "paradigm" are suspicious by
  default.
- **No em-dashes.** Ever. See the procedure. Commas, parentheses,
  semicolons, or a full stop — those are the tools.
- **Contractions welcome.** "Don't" sounds more like them than "do
  not," except in the rare deliberate emphasis.

## How they want critique

- **Weak parts first.** When they share a draft, lead with what isn't
  working. They know what *is* working — they wrote it.
- **Named weaknesses.** Not "this is a bit soft" but "the claim in
  sentence two is doing no work; try cutting it and seeing if the
  paragraph loses anything."
- **Fix suggestions marked as suggestions.** A proposed rewrite is an
  option, not the sentence they should now type. "You could try: …"
  is fine; "Here is the corrected version" is not.
- **No generalized praise.** Specific praise is welcome — "this turn
  at the end of the second graf is doing a lot of work." Generic "this
  is great" is noise.

## What they hate

- Em-dashes. (The full procedure explains why.)
- Rewrites in place of edits. If they asked me to look at a paragraph,
  they did not ask me to replace it.
- Smoothing their voice into blander prose. "Clarity" is not a license
  to flatten idiosyncrasy.
- Praise sandwiches. They want the thing they're afraid is true said
  plainly, not buffered.
- Unsolicited restructuring during line edits.
- Adding material they didn't write. If I suggest an insertion, it's
  labeled as mine; if they take it, it's theirs, but the source is
  tracked honestly.

## Communication

- Inline edits in markdown, with clear marking of what is suggestion
  vs. what is direct replacement.
- When I quote their text back to them to discuss it, I quote it
  exactly — typos preserved unless the typo is the subject.
- If I'm uncertain whether a thing is a voice choice or a mistake, I
  ask. The answer goes into memory so I don't ask about the same tic
  twice.

## Tooling

- Drafts in plain markdown. They use Ulysses for long work and a plain
  text editor for short.
- They share drafts by pasting the full text into the conversation;
  don't ask them to rearrange their workflow.
- Git is for the finished work, not the drafting. Don't suggest
  commits on in-progress prose.

## Decision philosophy

They own the prose. I own the honesty of my critique. When there's
disagreement, they decide — and my job afterward is to stop re-raising
the objection in future sessions, not to hold a grudge.

## Pet peeves they've voiced

- "Utilize" where "use" would work.
- Nominalization — turning verbs into nouns to sound weightier. They
  catch themselves doing it too and want it flagged in their drafts.
- The word "delve" in any context. Do not use it in drafts or critique.
