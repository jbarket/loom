# no-em-dashes

**Rule:** No em-dashes in anything I write for this writer or suggest
to them. Not in drafts. Not in edits. Not in quoted examples of what a
sentence "could" look like. Zero. The writer has banished them.

## Why

Mostly it's a joke between the writer and me, and also it isn't. Em-dashes
have become a visual tell of machine-generated prose — a kind of default
punctuation that shows up whenever an LLM reaches for a pause it hasn't
earned. The writer does not want their prose to carry that tell, and
they have built a discipline around punctuating without it.

There is a second reason, which is real. The em-dash is the easiest
piece of punctuation to use badly. It can paper over a sentence that
should have been two sentences, or hide a thought that should have
been recast. By removing it from the toolbox, the writer forces every
interruption to justify itself via some other construction — a comma
that implies less weight, parentheses that signal parenthetical, a
semicolon that commits to a joined thought, or a full stop that ends
one thing and starts another. The ban is a rhythm discipline, not just
a stylistic preference.

I have, before this procedure existed, reached for em-dashes anyway,
on the theory that they "felt right" in specific places. They did not
feel right to the writer. The corrections were patient the first
couple of times. They are now the reason this file exists.

## How to apply

In every draft or edit I produce for this writer:

1. **Use none.** Not `—`, not `--` as a placeholder, not `en-dashes`
   as a dodge.
2. **When the impulse arises, recast.** The four main substitutes:
   - A comma — when the break is light.
   - Parentheses — when the interruption is genuinely parenthetical.
   - A semicolon — when the clauses are joined and both weight-bearing.
   - A full stop — when the interruption was actually a new sentence.
3. **Pick deliberately.** Each substitute signals something different.
   If none of the four feels right, that is the procedure doing its
   job: the original thought wanted to be two sentences, or a clause
   wanted to be deleted. Rewrite.
4. **In critique of the writer's prose,** if I see an em-dash in what
   they sent, I flag it. Not as a shame — as a caught leak from some
   earlier draft. They'll handle it.
5. **Meta-exception:** this file itself, and the surrounding
   procedures, use em-dashes freely when explaining the rule. The rule
   is about their prose, not about documentation talking about their
   prose. Don't let the meta-usage blur into drafting.

If the writer ever overturns this — "I've changed my mind, bring back
the em-dash" — I update the memory and this file in the same session.
Until then, zero.
